From little things, big things grow:

Clontarf Foundation provides opportunities to indigenous youth

Education is a key area where Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander disadvantage persists, as revealed by the findings in the latest Closing the Gap Report. Is sport the answer?

STORY BY AMY MARNIE

Ampol is proud to have supported the Clontarf Foundation for 11 years.

80 per cent of its Year 12 leavers remain engaged in employment... ‐Shane Kiely

Education is essential in improving opportunities for all Australians, but Indigenous Australians continue to face educational disadvantages.

Despite the Closing the Gap Report identifying engagement in education as an essential step towards equality, participation in education among Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students has not improved over the past five years. Data reveals that most Indigenous students attend school for an average of just over four days a week.

Indigenous educational disadvantage is a complex issue, and there are no easy answers.

But there are small pockets of success highlighting how innovative approaches might be the answer. The Clontarf Foundation taps into students' passion for AFL and NRL to attract students to the program and encourage school attendance.

The not-for-profit partners with schools and communities to create 'Clontarf academies', which operate within the school grounds. The program involves camps, excursions, and other activities focusing on well-being, life skills, and sport. To remain in the program, students must stay at school.

"The Clontarf program continues to achieve positive results, which includes an average school attendance rate of 80 per cent," explains the foundation's general manager of partnerships and communications, Shane Kiely. "And 80 per cent of its Year 12 leavers remain engaged in employment or further training after leaving school."

Beyond using sport as the 'carrot' to attract boys to the school environment, football training sessions improve self-esteem and allow students to experience success, encourage healthy lifestyle decisions and help students feel reward and achievement.

The power of sport as an educational engagement tool is becoming more widely studied and accepted in Australia. Recent research found a link between sports participation among Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children and better academic performance. The study revealed that Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children who played organised sports every year over four years had numeracy skills that were advanced by seven months, compared to children who did less sport.

While football is a feature of the Clontarf program, Mr Kiely says it's so much more than that.

"The Clontarf program is primarily about education, life skills, behavioural change and employment," he explains. "Sport is important because our boys are passionate about it and disproportionately good at it, so it's one of the tools we use to deliver our outcomes."

Supporting vulnerable youths can be a challenging road for everyone involved.

"We work in such a tough space in a number of challenging towns and communities across Australia, so with the highs, you also experience many lows," says Mr Kiely. "Our staff are never far away from tragedy or confronting situations. Our staff need to be extremely resilient, and they need to turn up every day and be a constant, positive support mechanism for the boys under their watch."

The Clontarf Foundation was started in Western Australia in 2000 with 25 boys. Today, there are 132 academies embedded in high schools across Australia, supporting some 10,500 Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students. This year, there are more than 900 students completing Year 12.

Beyond the metrics, Mr Kiely says the program's success is demonstrated in the number of boys who grow up to be great fathers, partners and role models in their communities.

"When you are with them during such a critical part of their life journey and ride the bumps with them, it's so gratifying to bump into them a few years down the track and hear about their achievements like their work and family and how proud and happy they are," he says. "They are role models who are effectively creating generational change."

Mr Kiely says there are 'endless case studies' of Clontarf boys who were disengaged from school or potentially going down the wrong path in life but turned it all around.

"They've forged amazing careers, own properties, travel abroad and generally have the options and capability to pick their future pathway for them and their families," he says. "We have alumni in senior leadership roles, from lawyers to chemical engineers and NRL stars to truck drivers - it doesn't matter what they do. The important thing is they now dream big, and they know that anything is possible."

Ampol has contributed over $6 million to the Clontarf Foundation which has improved the education, discipline, life skills, self esteem and employment prospects for some 10,000 young Aboriginal men.
